The proposal

Medicine the way it was meant to work

A physician lives in Brookfield. His home is the clinic. Two nurses work alongside him. You come when you need care — for your annual checkup, a sick kid at 8 a.m., something that has been nagging for weeks. You get seen. You go home. No bill arrives.

You already paid — the same way you pay for the fire department, the road crew, the library that has been running since 1791. Not because every house will catch fire, but because having the capacity matters, and the cost of not having it is too high when you need it.

This is not a program handed down from Montpelier. This is Brookfield deciding what Brookfield is worth.

⊕

Primary care

Sick visits, annual checkups, managing ongoing conditions like diabetes or high blood pressure, stitches and minor procedures — on site, same week.

◎

X-ray and lab work

Your doctor takes and reads your X-rays and EKGs in the building. Basic blood and urine tests done on site. Most answers before you leave.

○

Overnight observation

Two beds for patients who need watching overnight but do not need a hospital 40 minutes away.

◑

Specialist referral

When you need a cardiologist, an orthopedist, or a surgeon, your doctor makes the call and stays involved. You are not handed a phone number and left to figure it out.

◐

Preventive care

Cancer screenings, blood pressure checks, cholesterol panels, vaccines if you want them. Catching things early costs far less than treating them late.

◉

No bill. Ever.

No copay. No deductible. No prior authorization. No surprise bill six weeks later. Your levy covers it.

How it works

Built differently, on purpose

01

The town owns it

The clinic, the building, the equipment — owned by the Town of Brookfield. Not by the physician. Not by the selectboard. Not by any hospital system or corporation. Operational control cannot be transferred to any outside entity without a town referendum.

02

The doctor is paid a salary — nothing else

Your doctor earns the same whether he sees you once this year or twelve times. He has no reason to order tests you do not need, refer unnecessarily, or rush you out the door. He gets paid to keep Brookfield healthy. That is the job.

03

An elected board sets the standards

Five members elected at Town Meeting. Two-year terms. Two-term limit, then a term off. Holds the physician accountable to outcomes, not billing targets. If the board is not doing its job, you vote them out.

04

Your officials use it too

Every elected official and selectboard member who lives in Brookfield gets their primary care here. No separate plan, no private option while in office. When the people making decisions about the clinic are also patients in it, their interests and yours are the same.

05

Upkeep done by Brookfielders

Maintenance and repairs preferentially go to residents of five years or more. Any contract over $2,000 requires competitive bids. Board members with family or financial ties to a bidder recuse, recorded in the minutes. No vendor can dominate the spend without public review.

06

The books are open

An annual public health report is presented at Town Meeting every year, alongside an independent audit. Spending line by line. Health outcomes for the town. No figure is reported on fewer than 11 people, so no resident is identifiable in the aggregate. If something looks off, you will see it.

Startup costs — X-ray machine, exam tables, lab equipment, building work — are a one-time expense of $80,000 to $200,000, funded through a town bond or rural infrastructure grant. Not in the annual levy.

Divided across Brookfield's 692 households:

$43 to $55 per household per month

Vermont's average individual health insurance premium runs $500 to $700 per person per month. We are not replacing that — not yet. We are making your first line of care cost almost nothing, and putting it ten minutes away.

Medicare and Medicaid reimbursements for enrolled patients offset the levy over time. Voluntary supplemental contributions from residents who choose to give more reduce the levy further.

Governance and protection

The safeguards, at three levels

The commons is protected at three legal altitudes so no single capture event — a corrupt doctor, a patronage board, an outside operator, or a snowy-Tuesday budget revolt — can unwind it.

Charter level

Hardest to change. Requires town vote and legislative approval. Protects the structural DNA.

No private ownership. Salaried physician model. No third-party billing. Control transfer requires referendum. Original purpose statement.

Ordinance level

Changeable by town vote at Town Meeting. Protects the operating rules.

Levy floor and locked five-year term. Conflict-of-interest and bidding rules. Report privacy standard. Officials-enroll provision.

Contract level

Board administers. Public reviews annually. Protects daily operations.

Three-year physician term. External chart review at renewal. Locum coverage arrangement. Vendor list and audit.

Time horizons

What the commons looks like over time

The doctor does not live a hundred years. The town does. Each horizon names what must hold, and what protects it.

5 yr

First locked term ends

Levy floor expires; town re-authorizes. First major equipment item begins drawing against reserve. Annual audit baseline established. Physician contract has cycled once.

10 yr

Three physician cycles complete

Board has fully turned over once under term limits. External review history available for renewal decisions. Original founders are not running the show.

25 yr

Mandatory charter re-ratification

The town affirms or amends the original purpose statement by public vote. The covenant is not assumed — it is renewed. Drift is interrupted by deliberate choice.

50 yr

Facility replacement funded

Sinking fund accumulated over the prior decades pays for full facility replacement. Standing affiliation with a Vermont residency program keeps the physician pipeline alive — Brookfield is a known training site, not a cold recruit each cycle.

100 yr

The covenant, preserved or retired

Successive 25-year ratifications either preserve the commons or formally retire it. Either outcome is the town's deliberate choice, not drift. Brookfield's grandchildren own this decision.

Your rights

What this clinic will and will not do

This is a community commons, not a government mandate. Your medical decisions are yours. Your records are protected by law. No one runs this clinic but Brookfield.

The clinic is for Brookfield residents. People from outside town are not seen for routine care — there are other clinics for that. The one exception is a genuine emergency. If someone collapses on a neighbor's property or gets hurt in town, we do not turn them away. We stabilize them and get them to the right place.

The clinic will

  • Treat every Brookfield resident regardless of insurance or ability to pay
  • Keep your records private under federal law — HIPAA applies the same as anywhere
  • Offer screenings and vaccines; never require them as a condition of care
  • Refer you to a specialist when that is what you need, and follow up
  • Open its books at every Town Meeting — spending, outcomes, every line
  • Provide emergency stabilization to anyone, regardless of where they live
  • Be governed by an elected board with term limits and conflict-of-interest rules
  • Suppress any figure in the public report derived from fewer than 11 people

The clinic will not

  • See non-residents for routine or ongoing care
  • Share your records with any government agency without your written consent or a court order
  • Require any treatment, vaccine, or procedure as a condition of receiving care
  • Be owned, managed, or directed by the state or federal government
  • Allow the physician, board members, or any official to hold ownership in the clinic, equipment, or supplies
  • Be sold or operationally handed to a private operator without a town referendum
  • Follow an insurance company's treatment protocols
  • Give voluntary contributors preference in scheduling, care, or board influence
