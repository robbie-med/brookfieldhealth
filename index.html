Manifesto

The covenant

Medicine in this country has become an industry. It bills you in fragments, gatekeeps your access through middlemen who never see you, and asks your doctor to render eight minutes of attention before charging your insurer for fourteen codes. The relationship between patient and physician — the oldest professional relationship in civilization — has been severed from the act of payment and re-stitched to a clearinghouse. What we get is not medicine. It is throughput.

Brookfield is a town. Towns are older than industries. Towns know how to pay together for the things no one can afford alone. We pay together for the fire department because fire does not negotiate. We pay together for the road crew because winter does not negotiate. We pay together for the school because childhood does not negotiate.

Illness does not negotiate either.

The town hires a doctor. The doctor takes care of the town. The town watches what the doctor does. That is the whole arrangement.

This proposal removes three things from the practice of primary care in Brookfield: the insurer, the billing relationship, and the private owner. Every dollar that funds the commons comes from Brookfielders. Every decision about how it operates is made by Brookfielders. Every patient who walks through the door is a Brookfielder. The physician is salaried. He cannot be paid more for ordering more tests. He cannot be paid less for taking more time. He cannot be sold to a private equity firm because the town owns the building.

What is left, when those things are removed, is the original act: a doctor and a patient, in a room, taking the time it actually takes. That is the covenant. The proposal exists to protect it.

What follows is the operational framework. It is detailed because the covenant deserves to be defended with detail. It is honest about its weak points because honesty at the start is cheaper than failure later. Read it. Bring objections. We have already engaged the most serious ones and you will find our answers below.

— Brookfielders for Brookfield

At a glance

What you actually get

A physician lives in Brookfield. His residence and the clinic share a building. Two nurses work alongside him. You come when you need care. You get seen. You go home. No bill arrives.

⊕

Primary care

Sick visits, annual checkups, managing diabetes or high blood pressure, stitches and minor procedures — on site, same week.

◎

X-ray and lab work

Digital X-ray, EKG, basic blood and urine tests done on site. Your doctor reads the films himself. Most answers before you leave.

○

Extended observation

Two recovery beds for short-stay observation during clinic hours — IV hydration, post-procedure recovery, watching a worrisome patient for several hours. Not overnight. Patients needing overnight care are transferred.

◑

Specialist coordination

When you need a cardiologist, an orthopedist, or a surgeon, your doctor makes the call and stays involved. You are not handed a phone number.

◐

Preventive care

Cancer screenings, blood pressure checks, cholesterol panels, vaccines if you want them. Quarterly town health classes at the library.

◉

No bill. Ever.

No copay. No deductible. No prior authorization. No surprise bill six weeks later. Your levy covers it.

The framework

Six principles, each load-bearing

The structure is built so no single failure unwinds the whole. Each principle below is enforced at a specific legal level — charter, ordinance, or contract.

01

The town owns it

The clinic, the building, the equipment — owned by the Town of Brookfield. Not by the physician, the board, or any outside system. Operational control cannot be transferred to any outside entity without a town referendum — this covers not just sale, but management contracts and partnerships that would shift authority over hiring, clinical protocols, billing, or records.

02

The physician is salaried — full stop

One salary. No fee-for-service component. No bonuses for volume or productivity. No competing private practice. The salary is set by the board within a published band and tied to credentials, FQHC status, and tenure — not to how many patients are seen.

03

The board governs, peers review

A five-member elected board sets policy, approves the budget, and holds the physician contract. Clinical performance is reviewed by an outside physician peer reviewer through the regional partnership — the board reviews the peer review, not the charts. Two-year terms, two-term limit, then a term off.

04

Officials enroll for primary care

Elected officials and selectboard members residing in Brookfield register as patients for primary care. Existing specialist and chronic-care relationships with outside providers are preserved — this is about shared standing in the routine care system, not coercion in specialty treatment.

05

Brookfielders maintain it, with guardrails

Building maintenance preferentially goes to residents of five years or more. Any contract over $2,000 requires competitive bids. Board members with family or financial ties to a bidder recuse, recorded in minutes. No single vendor can exceed 30% of annual maintenance spend without public review.

06

The books are open and the report cannot identify you

Annual public health report presented at Town Meeting alongside an independent financial audit. Spending line by line. Health outcomes for the town. No figure is reported on fewer than eleven people. No geographic breakdown below the town level. Sensitive conditions are reported only at the whole-town level.

Operational spec

How the commons actually runs

This is the operational layer beneath the principles. Each block below answers a specific operational question. If you are evaluating whether this is real or aspirational, read this section.

The physician

Recruitment · Salary · Contract · Coverage

Salary band: $120,000 to $180,000, set by the board within published criteria. The lower end fits a physician for whom the model itself — autonomy, no billing, salaried clarity, the relational practice — is part of the compensation. The upper end allows competitive recruitment if the lower end fails to attract a qualified candidate within six months.

Contract: three-year initial term, renewable. Renewal is the default; non-renewal requires specific findings from the peer review and the board, documented in writing. Severance and locum bridge are pre-funded.

Housing: the physician's residence is the residential portion of the town-owned clinic building, leased under a term that runs with the contract. When the contract ends, the housing arrangement ends. The physician may instead live elsewhere in Brookfield and lease the clinical space only — the board decides per candidate.

Coverage when away: standing locum agreement with regional partner (target: Gifford Medical Center, Randolph) provides credentialed physicians for vacation, illness, CME, and military duty. Budget supports four weeks of locum coverage per year. The same partnership provides 14-day backfill capacity if the seat goes vacant — the town is not waiting 12 to 24 months for a new physician.

Malpractice: covered under the Federal Tort Claims Act once FQHC status is secured (target: year two). Until then, the budget carries a $12,000 line for commercial professional liability coverage.

The nurses

Two RN positions · Day shifts only

Two registered nurses, town employees. Salary range $60,000 to $75,000 each. Coverage is day shifts only — there is no overnight nursing because there is no overnight inpatient operation. Nurses cover triage, chronic disease monitoring, vaccine administration, blood draws, point-of-care lab testing, and follow-up.

One nurse is always present during clinic hours. When the physician is on locum coverage and a substitute is in the building, both nurses are present.

What the clinic does — and does not

Scope of service

The clinic provides primary care, basic diagnostics, minor procedures, and short-stay observation during clinic hours. It does not operate inpatient beds. It does not provide emergency department services. It does not run an in-house pharmacy.

The two beds are recovery and observation beds, not inpatient beds. They may be used for: IV hydration, post-procedure recovery, observation of a patient whose condition is uncertain during clinic hours, comfort space for an elderly patient awaiting transport. Any patient still requiring monitoring at the end of clinic hours is transferred to a hospital.

Emergencies are stabilized regardless of residency and transferred to the appropriate facility by ambulance. The clinic is not a 911 destination.

Billing and reimbursement

Patients pay nothing · Public payers reimburse the town

No patient ever receives a bill from the clinic. No copay, no deductible, no balance bill, no surprise bill. The levy covers it.

Medicare and Medicaid reimbursements for enrolled beneficiaries are billed by a part-time contract biller (target $8,000/year, pursued under FQHC enhanced reimbursement once status is secured). These reimbursements flow into the commons fund and offset the levy. Private insurers are not billed for primary care services at the commons — that is the structural commitment.

Why this distinction matters: billing your insurer would put the clinic back in the position of negotiating with a third party that doesn't see you. Public-payer reimbursement, by contrast, is the town claiming money that is statutorily due to the town for caring for its Medicare and Medicaid residents.

Records and privacy

HIPAA · CLIA · Small-town confidentiality

Federal HIPAA applies to all records. CLIA certification covers all on-site lab testing. Records are kept in an open-source EMR hosted locally on a town-owned server, with encrypted off-site backup.

The physician signs a confidentiality covenant at hire that explicitly addresses the small-town context — knowing patients socially heightens the duty, never lessens it. The covenant is a contractual obligation, not a policy the board can wave.

Patients have the right to obtain primary care from any other provider at any time, at their own expense and on their own insurance. The levy is not contingent on using the commons.

Insurance interaction for working residents

HSA-compatible as of January 2026

For most working residents who carry employer-sponsored or marketplace insurance, the commons is an addition to that coverage, not a replacement. The levy supplements your existing premium.

Federal law as of January 2026 (One Big Beautiful Bill Act) makes the levy a qualified medical expense under HSA rules, paid with pre-tax dollars on an HSA-eligible plan. The board provides a written explanatory letter for your benefits administrator on request.

The board commits to working with major Brookfield-area employers — the school district, regional employers with Brookfield workforce — to ensure HSA-eligible HDHP options exist where they do not. We cannot force any insurer to lower premiums. We can demonstrate over time that residents with a local primary care doctor cost the insurance system less, which is the argument that eventually moves rates.

For Medicare and Medicaid recipients, the reimbursement flows back to the commons and reduces the levy for everyone. For people who pay full ACA premiums with no employer coverage, the levy is a real net add until total healthcare costs come down through other means.

Governance and protection

The safeguards, at three levels

The commons is protected at three legal altitudes so no single capture event — a corrupt doctor, a patronage board, an outside operator, or a snowy-Tuesday budget revolt — can unwind it.

Charter level

Hardest to change. Requires town vote and legislative approval. Protects the structural DNA.

No private ownership. Salaried physician model. No third-party billing of patients. Control transfer requires referendum. Original purpose statement.

Ordinance level

Changeable by town vote at Town Meeting. Protects the operating rules.

Levy floor, five-year locked term, sinking fund, conflicts and bidding, report privacy standard, officials-enroll provision, wind-down clause.

Contract level

Board administers. Public reviews annually. Protects daily operations.

Three-year physician term. External peer review at renewal. Locum and partnership arrangements. Vendor list and independent audit.

Time horizons

What persists, and what gets renewed

The doctor does not live a hundred years. The town does. Each horizon names what must hold, and what protects it. The structure persists by being deliberately re-chosen, not by being assumed.

5 yr

First locked term ends

Levy floor expires; town re-authorizes for another five years. First major equipment item begins drawing against reserve. Annual audit baseline established. Physician contract has cycled once. FQHC status is pursued and secured; malpractice line drops to zero under FTCA.

10 yr

Three physician cycles complete

Board has fully turned over once under term limits. External peer review history available for renewal decisions. Original founders are not running the show. Residency program affiliation, if pursued, has placed at least one trainee.

25 yr

Mandatory charter re-ratification

The town affirms or amends the original purpose statement by public vote. The covenant is not assumed by inheritance — each generation chooses it. Drift is interrupted by deliberate choice.

50 yr

Facility replacement funded

Sinking fund accumulated over the prior decades pays for full facility replacement. Standing affiliation with a Vermont residency program keeps the physician pipeline alive — Brookfield is a known training site, not a cold recruit each cycle.

100 yr

The covenant, preserved or retired

Successive 25-year ratifications either preserve the commons or formally retire it. Either outcome is the town's deliberate choice, not drift. Brookfield's grandchildren own this decision.

Objections engaged

What the critics got right

Townspeople have read this proposal and pushed back. Some of their points landed; we changed the plan. Some were partially right; we clarified. Some were lazy. Here is the running record.

Objection · Accepted · Plan revised

The original budget had no malpractice line

That was a real error. Primary care physician malpractice insurance runs $8,000 to $15,000 a year in Vermont, more with procedures and observation work. The budget now carries a $12,000 line for commercial coverage.

This line drops to zero once the commons obtains FQHC designation (target year two), because Federal Tort Claims Act coverage applies to FQHC physicians for their clinical work. Pursuing FQHC status is therefore a real budget priority, not a nice-to-have.

Objection · Accepted · Plan revised

Overnight observation beds are a regulatory and liability bomb

This was the sharpest critique and it landed. Operating inpatient beds with a single sleeping physician and no overnight nursing is a category of risk this clinic cannot absorb.

The plan now provides short-stay observation during clinic hours only. The two beds remain — used for IV hydration, post-procedure recovery, and watching a patient for several hours before deciding to transfer. Any patient still needing monitoring at the end of clinic hours is transferred to a hospital. There is no overnight inpatient operation.

Objection · Accepted · Plan revised

"$10,000 in locum coverage" was inadequate

That was roughly two weeks. A physician needs more — vacation, illness, continuing medical education, Guard duty. The line is now $24,000, supporting four weeks per year of credentialed locum coverage through the regional partnership.

The same partnership commits to providing a credentialed physician within 14 days if the seat goes vacant — addressing the single-point-of-failure risk directly. Brookfield is not waiting 12 to 24 months for a new doctor.

Objection · Accepted · Plan revised

"No third-party billing" and "Medicare reimbursement" contradicted each other

Correct. The clarification: patients never receive a bill. The clinic bills Medicare and Medicaid for enrolled beneficiaries — which is the town claiming money statutorily due to it for caring for its public-payer residents. A part-time contract biller handles this ($8,000/year line). Private insurers are never billed.

Objection · Accepted · Plan revised

The startup capital range was a shrug, not a plan

A range of "$80,000 to $200,000" was not a budget. The plan now itemizes startup capital to $150,000 firm, with a 20% contingency built in. See the budget section for the line-by-line breakdown.

Objection · Accepted · Plan revised

"Officials must enroll" was too broad and coercive

The provision is now narrower: officials enroll for primary care. Existing specialist and chronic-care relationships are preserved. A selectboard member managing cancer with a specialist in Burlington is not required to disrupt that care. The shared standing applies to routine primary care, where it matters most for accountability and where it costs participants least.

Objection · Partially accepted · Clarified

"The board cannot evaluate physician competence"

Correct that a lay board cannot do peer review. The plan was never that they would. Clinical performance is evaluated by an outside physician through the regional partnership — a credentialed peer reviewer who reviews charts and reports back. The board reviews the peer review, not the charts. This is how hospital credentialing committees actually work.

The website previously said "external chart review" without explaining who performed it. That ambiguity is now closed.

Objection · Partially accepted · Clarified

"A doctor willing to work for $120k is suspicious"

The salary band is now $120,000 to $180,000, set by the board within published criteria. The lower end is for a physician for whom the model itself — autonomy, no billing, salaried clarity, the relational practice — is part of the compensation. The upper end allows competitive recruitment if the model alone does not draw a qualified candidate. The point of the model is to find someone who would do this anyway, not to find the cheapest body.

Objection · Partially accepted · Clarified

"Insurance interaction is a legal knot"

It is, and a website cannot settle individual benefits questions. The plan now commits to providing a written explanatory letter for any resident's benefits administrator on request, and to working with major Brookfield-area employers to ensure HSA-eligible HDHP options exist.

The board will not pretend the plan eliminates an individual's total healthcare cost. The argument is that primary care is moved out of the insurance system entirely, with measurable downstream savings over time as fewer ER visits and better chronic disease management reduce claims.

Objection · Rejected

"100-year planning is fiction"

This misunderstands the structure. The 25-year re-ratification does not assume that Brookfield in 2052 can design healthcare for Brookfield in 2152. It commits each generation to deliberately choose or formally retire the covenant of local ownership and accountability. The specific operations adapt; the principles re-ratify. Without the re-ratification, the structure drifts into irrelevance and gets quietly captured. With it, every generation owns the decision.

Objection · Rejected

"The financial case only holds if you ignore that total costs are unchanged"

The financial case is not "this saves you money on insurance." The financial case is: primary care, the most-used and most-cost-effective layer of medicine, is moved out of the insurance system entirely, paid for at-cost by the town, with no copay or deductible gating access. For most working residents, the levy is paid with pre-tax HSA dollars and is therefore a 20% to 25% smaller bite than its face value suggests. Over time, fewer ER visits and better chronic management reduce downstream insurance costs. We do not pretend this is a free lunch. We do claim the math works for most households and we will publish the data showing it.

Your rights

What this clinic will and will not do

This is a community commons, not a government mandate. Your medical decisions are yours. Your records are protected by law. No one runs this clinic but Brookfield.

The clinic is for Brookfield residents. People from outside town are not seen for routine care — there are other clinics for that. The one exception is a genuine emergency: anyone gets stabilized and transferred. That is what any decent person would do, and what this clinic will do.

The clinic will

  • Treat every Brookfield resident regardless of insurance or ability to pay
  • Keep your records private under federal law — HIPAA applies the same as anywhere
  • Offer screenings and vaccines; never require them as a condition of care
  • Refer you to a specialist when that is what you need, and follow up
  • Open its books at every Town Meeting — spending, outcomes, every line
  • Provide emergency stabilization to anyone, regardless of where they live
  • Be governed by an elected board with term limits and conflict-of-interest rules
  • Suppress any figure in the public report derived from fewer than 11 people
  • Publish a written letter for your benefits administrator on request

The clinic will not

  • See non-residents for routine or ongoing care
  • Operate inpatient or overnight beds
  • Share your records with any government agency without your written consent or a court order
  • Require any treatment, vaccine, or procedure as a condition of receiving care
  • Be owned, managed, or directed by the state or federal government
  • Allow the physician, board members, or any official to hold ownership in the clinic, equipment, or supplies
  • Be sold or operationally handed to a private operator without a town referendum
  • Bill private insurers or follow insurance company treatment protocols
  • Give voluntary contributors preference in scheduling, care, or board influence
